In a lot of companies, the appearance of being right and decisive oftentimes eclipses the desire to find a better solution. Talking to customers gets treated like a checkbox instead of the art and practice that it really is.

A huge amount of management pay depends on boosting shareholder value and being evaluated every quarter. Those incentives have created conditions in which it's nearly impossible to invest in long-term R&D strategies that take time to pay off.

Funding the kind of foundational research that real breakthroughs are built on. The transistor, the internet, and even today's AI all came out of long-horizon research that no quarterly earnings call would have tolerated.

Jack Welch called shareholder value the dumbest idea in the world. Warren Buffett's critique centers on the short-term, quarter-to-quarter pressure current governance rules create. In 2019 the Business Roundtable walked back the shareholder-primacy model too.

The net effect is that we've made ourselves mechanical, exactly the kind of work now at risk of being automated by the very AI that promises to unlock innovation.

Because maximizing what AI promises will not happen if we simply develop frontier models. It happens once we actually create useful solutions, products, and better systems using AI's capabilities.
