The yard sale simulation isn't a metaphor in your story — it could be the literal mechanism of his radicalization, and it would be more powerful for being real. Bruce Boghosian's model at Tufts demonstrates through actual probability mathematics that in any system of random wealth exchange, even with perfectly fair rules, wealth concentrates at the top inevitably and _cannot be recovered from the bottom without external forced redistribution_. The bottom doesn't fail because it didn't try. The math runs against it by design of how probability compounds at scale.

The moment your leader understands this — really understands it, not as an abstraction but as a closed loop — is the moment the world changes for him. He wasn't looking for a reason to burn things. He was looking for a mechanism that _didn't_ require burning things. The discovery is that he couldn't find one. Not for lack of trying. The feedback loops that historically forced elites to make concessions — labor organizing, democratic politics, investigative journalism — have been defunded, defanged, or captured. There's no internal corrective mechanism left.

He didn't want to be right about this. That's what makes him tragic.

### The Yard Sale Model: The Math Is Real

Bruce Boghosian is a professor of mathematics at Tufts, and his yard sale model isn't a political argument — it's a formal kinetic wealth exchange model derived from the same mathematical machinery used in statistical physics. The mechanism: two agents transact, one wins, one loses, the winner's stake scales with their existing wealth. Run millions of such transactions and watch what happens. Even with _perfectly random_ outcomes — no cheating, no bias, pure chance — wealth concentrates relentlessly at the top, and the bottom cannot recover without external forced redistribution.now.tufts+1

Boghosian has described the model as an "X-ray" — not a messy representation of the real world, but something that strips the noise away to reveal the underlying skeleton. The skeleton shows oligarchy as a natural mathematical attractor. When his team adjusted the model to allow for negative wealth and ran it against actual U.S. wealth distribution data from 1989 to 2016, the model fit the empirical data _remarkably faithfully_. This isn't conjecture. The math matches the world.pudding+1

What this means for your leader: the moment he encounters this, he's not reading an opinion. He's reading a proof. The poverty trap isn't a policy failure that better policy could fix. It's a structural outcome baked into how wealth compounds. That distinction is the difference between a reform target and a closed loop.

### The AI Commodity Divide: Already Happening

Sam Altman has written since at least 2013 that technology makes wealth inequality worse by giving people leverage and _compounding differences in ability and amount of work_ — that where once superior ability had linear effects on wealth, technology makes the effect exponential. What he articulated as a theory in 2013 is arriving as practice now.[[blog.samaltman](https://blog.samaltman.com/technology-and-wealth-inequality)]​

The knowledge divide is real and accelerating. AI doesn't distribute evenly — it **amplifies**. The same tool in the hands of someone with capital, context, and access to premium systems produces exponential returns. In the hands of someone without those things, it produces noise or worse — ad-driven, biased models optimized for engagement rather than accuracy. As one economic analyst put it bluntly: _"AI does not guarantee progress. It guarantees amplification. Amplification increases both return and ruin. The middle thins. The gap expands."_[[linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/stanbeloushko_ai-that-can-turn-100k-into-1m-isnt-an-activity-7429136597234847744-j1sW)]​

Applied to your story's world: the enclaves don't just have more money. They have better intelligence — literally. Their AI models are tuned, clean, uncompromised. The rebels are working with whatever they can get access to, which is hobbled by design. The information asymmetry is structural, not incidental.

### Structural Unemployment: Not Cyclical, Not Recoverable

This is the most important and least acknowledged piece. Every past wave of technological displacement — looms, manufacturing automation, computerization — was eventually absorbed. Workers retrained. New categories emerged. The argument for AI being different is now well-evidenced and mainstream.

Block's 2026 layoffs were described by analysts as the clearest signal yet: _"A team that previously employed 50 people does not need to rebuild to 30 when the economy rebounds; it can maintain a smaller workforce because AI now performs the tasks that were once necessary. This productivity enhancement — and the resulting job displacement — appears to be a permanent fixture."_ The Federal Reserve can cut rates to address cyclical demand shocks. There is no interest rate cut that fixes structural displacement.finance.yahoo+1

What makes this specifically relevant to your story is the _speed_ differential. Manufacturing automation replaced manual labor gradually over decades. AI is compressing the same transformations into months, faster than any retraining infrastructure can operate at scale. The people displaced aren't failing because they didn't try to adapt. The adaptation timeline is longer than the displacement timeline. The math is running against them the same way the yard sale model runs against the bottom.[[traxtech](https://www.traxtech.com/ai-in-supply-chain/labor-market-turbulence-exposes-ais-double-edged-impact-on-employment)]​

### The Institutional Capture Problem

This is where it closes into a loop for your leader. The standard historical correctives — labor unions, democratic redistribution, investigative journalism — all require institutional leverage that has been systematically eroded. One Reddit analysis from early March 2026 frames it directly: _"This situation is not a recession, nor is it a traditional depression. It represents a permanent structural shift in the dynamics between labor and capital, occurring more rapidly than any existing human institution can effectively manage."_[[reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/ArtificialInteligence/comments/1rm25px/i_spent_months_building_a_case_for_why_the_ai/)]​

The government policy toolkit was designed for cyclical disruption. Retraining programs, unemployment benefits, rate adjustments — these are all cyclical tools applied to a structural problem. Your leader would understand this not as a conspiracy but as something worse: a system that has simply _outrun the capacity of its corrective mechanisms_, and where the people who could fund those mechanisms have every financial incentive not to.traxtech+1

### What This Means for the Story's Credibility

The fictional near-future you're building doesn't require extrapolation. It requires _continuation_. The yard sale math already describes 2026. The AI amplification gap is already opening. The structural displacement is already happening faster than institutions can absorb. Your leader doesn't need to be prophetic — he needs to be someone who read what was available, ran the logic forward, and arrived somewhere he didn't want to go.

## Who John Brown Was
He was a deeply religious man, shaped by Calvinist theology, who became consumed by the conviction that slavery was the nation's greatest sin and that decades of peaceful resistance had accomplished nothing. Where other abolitionists — including Frederick Douglass and William Lloyd Garrison — argued for moral persuasion, Brown thought that was, in his words, "all talk". He concluded that violence was not only justified but *required*. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Brown_(abolitionist))
### What He Did
He first gained national attention during **Bleeding Kansas** in the 1850s, leading anti-slavery guerrilla fighters in a territorial civil war over whether Kansas would enter the Union as a slave state. Then, in October 1859, he led a raid on the federal armory at **Harpers Ferry, Virginia** with 21 men — 16 white, 5 Black — intending to seize weapons, arm enslaved people, and spark a rebellion that would spread across the South. The raid failed. He was captured, tried for treason, and hanged on December 2, 1859 — the first person executed for treason in U.S. history. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Brown_(abolitionist))
### Why He's Never Been Settled
That's the part relevant to your story. Brown was *right* about slavery being an irredeemable evil. He was *right* that peaceful channels had been exhausted — the Fugitive Slave Act, the courts, Congress had all been captured by slaveholding interests. His raid failed militarily but the outrage it sparked on both sides helped ignite the Civil War, which ended slavery four years after his death. Historians still argue: was he a saint, a terrorist, a martyr, a murderer? The answer is genuinely unresolved — and that unresolvability is the point. [britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Brown-American-abolitionist)
### Why He Matters for Your Leader
Brown didn't act from rage. He acted from *conclusion* — after decades of trying every other path. He was a man who helped 2,500 enslaved people escape via the Underground Railroad before he ever raised a gun. Violence wasn't his first instinct; it was his last one, arrived at after the evidence was in. He even opposed a fellow conspirator's suggestion to blow up Southern churches, saying *humanity precluded unnecessary bloodshed* — while simultaneously planning an armed uprising. That contradiction, held without apparent awareness, is exactly the kind of interior fracture your leader carries. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Brown_(abolitionist))

The reason your leader won't feel simple if you write him like Brown is that Brown never stopped being moral — he just arrived at a moral conclusion most people couldn't follow him to. Whether he was right is the question the story doesn't have to answer. It just has to hold it open honestly.

## The Mentor Figure

The mentor shouldn't be someone who radicalizes him. That would make him simple again — someone manipulated or led astray. The mentor should be someone who _tried every legitimate path before him_, and can show him exactly where each one closed. Maybe an economist. Maybe a former regulator. Maybe someone who spent thirty years inside institutions trying to reform them and is now very old and very quiet about what they learned. They don't tell him to fight. They don't tell him anything prescriptive. They just show him the map, and the map has no doors left in it.

The mentor might not even endorse what the leader ultimately does. They might be horrified by it. That tension — _I showed you the truth, not permission to do this_ — could be one of the story's most alive relationships

Albert Camus wrote The Rebel specifically about this problem — what separates the person who says no to a specific injustice and thereby affirms a human value, versus the one who says no to everything and tries to build from absolute zero. The first is a rebel. The second becomes something harder to defend. Camus' argument is that the rebel's no is the most human act — but total revolution, because it rejects everything, can become its own tyranny.

The John Brown parallel is worth sitting with. Deeply moral man. Absolute intellectual conviction about the evil of slavery — and he was right. Did real violence. Historians still argue about whether he was a saint or a terrorist or both. No one calls him simple. He's exactly the kind of figure where the verdict depends entirely on what you believe about when legitimate channels have truly closed.
