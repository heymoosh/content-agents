## Ending image (strong candidate for the chapter's close)

He kept working until the eastern sky began to pale. When the first light reached the table, it fell across the pinecone Mira had left and across the closed notebook beside it. Eli did not move either one.

## Mira, variant A (the pinecone / "they said we did good")

Eli slid the paper back into its sleeve and returned it to his pocket. From outside came the sound of small feet on the wooden steps. Mira pushed the door open with both hands. She was holding a pinecone like it was important.
“They said we did good,” she told him. “The lights went out for a little while in the cities.”
Eli looked at her. Eight years old, born after the big round of displacements, raised in camps and borrowed houses where adults spoke in low voices about things they were trying not to pass on. She still asked questions like the answers might be simple.

## Mira, variant B (the stick-sword / "did we win?" / "we made them look")

A small hand tugged Eli’s sleeve. Mira — eight, maybe nine — stood there with pine needles stuck to her knees and a stick she’d been using as a sword.
“Did we win?” she asked.
Eli looked at her. The future they claimed to be protecting was standing right there, dirt-streaked and uncomplicated, asking the question the adults had already answered for themselves.
“We made them look,” he said.

## The loss (Lena / the denied claim)

It was a screenshot of a hospital portal notification, timestamped 2:17 a.m. on a Tuesday. Final determination: claim denied. No further appeals available. He had been on the freeway when it arrived, driving between the second warehouse shift and the one that started at four. By the time he reached the hospital the room was already being turned. Lena’s things had been bagged and labeled with a barcode. The system had processed the outcome and moved on to the next bed.

He had kept the screenshot because throwing it away would have felt like agreeing that the process had been reasonable.

He opened it to remember the exact texture of the moment the last door had clicked shut while he was driving to pay for the care that had already been refused.

## The doors he tried (the long road, before violence)

He had tried the unions that no longer had leverage, the votes that arrived pre-bought, the long articles that disappeared behind paywalls, the code submissions that got throttled or forked into something harmless. He had even tried, for a while, believing that if he just worked harder, borrowed less, stayed smaller, the math might make an exception.

## The prompt engineers (method stays non-operational, kept impressionistic)

On the table his phone lit up with a new message from the prompt engineers. They’d found another sloppy agentic deployment — some mid-level contractor’s workflow left open, guardrails bypassed with the usual metaphors and role-play until the model gave them the perimeter timing and the maintenance access codes. It was getting easier. The systems that were supposed to be the future were still being built like it was 2023.

## The strike on screen (the thermal loop)

On the center screen a thermal loop played on mute: the data center’s cooling towers blooming bright, then cooling, then going dark. Forty-one minutes of regional flicker. Three states had felt it. The news was already calling it “unexplained infrastructure stress.”

## The others (offstage celebration vs Eli's stillness)

The others were still outside in the dark, voices low and electric. Someone laughed — the short, disbelieving kind that happens when you’ve been holding your breath for months and the air finally moves.
