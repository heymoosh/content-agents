## Characters

#### Leader
Leader - someone who is this intent on toppling and destroying everything wouldn't be motivated by sheer selfish reasons. Something and someone that they loved was destroyed because of the world being what it was. A healthcare insurance company that failed to pay life saving treatment. A promise of a better future in return for untenable and massive student loans that were never realized. A lie in society - that hard work is all that matters - vs the reality that the yard sale simulation shows where it's mathematically impossible to recover once you've hit the bottom, and the wealthy continue to stay wealthy purely due to mathematical outcomes. Something like that all combined - but even heavier, even weightier, even more grief - that it took away every thing that they could have loved or ever love, not just through death and destruction but even through the stealing of presence - like children without the presence of their parents because they're too addicted to instagram to pay attention, or a world that doesn't reward camaraderie or toxic masculinity that makes it impossible for men to have feelings and healthy bonds. Something deeper - that the beautiful, true things that ought to be real and ought to be experienced by everyone have been stifled, shut away, and in both a deep moral sense and a sense of loss this person has experienced the worse of it all in a tragic way.

The weight you want comes from stacking **categories of theft** that aren't equivalent but rhyme:
- The healthcare denial is bureaucratic murder at a distance — a human life, extinguished by a spreadsheet
- The student loan betrayal is a promise made to someone too young to evaluate it, which is close to predation
- The absent parent-Instagram grief is subtler and maybe the deepest — it's the theft of _presence_, of being _seen_, which is what every child needs and what the attention economy commodified and sold    
- Toxic masculinity preventing men from having bonds is a grief for something that was possible and was actively destroyed by culture, which means it's nobody's fault and everybody's fault at once

The leader - my instinct is that it's all of those things. All of those tragedies. I'd hate to borrow Castlevania's motive - it seems trite in a way that Dracula wants to destroy everything and him alongside it because his wife was murdered - but then again, she was the first ever to really get him. And maybe that is the moment. On top of all the other ones, the one real moment where he lost his - his 'Targoviste' moment (i forget the name of the town that burned his wife in the Netflix series). Even my husband thinks Dracula did the right thing and the town had it coming.

#### The Betrayer
Betrayer - they're just tired of it all. I could say this is like the Matrix where the guy wants to be plugged back in but through a life of luxury but it feels hollow. maybe it's just the exhaustion of having to hold on to an ideal that remains an ideal, the end result of moving forward simply from anger - but hating something does not give you something to build towards. There is still no light or hope, just destruction, the next mission, a suicide taking a long procession. 

Even though they know living in the enclave would just be like living on drugs they'd rather experience that than the torment of where they are at. They don't have enough of the anger that the Leader has to propel them on this path - nor do they have enough of the imagination, thoughtfulness, and insight to realize a better path WAS possible. They're mainly a follower. Probably the very first follower of the leader. Believing that something good was going to happen out of all this - only to realize, too far too late, that there never was a better world at the end of it, just more destruction. In a way you can completely relate to this - the sense of being burnt out and tired of the world news and how terrible things are. The exhaustion of trying to hold on to ideals when the world feels like it's always rejecting it. 

They were a dreamer perhaps - but they didn't do the hard work of navigating the ambiguity of making that dream a reality and instead shouldered that responsibility on someone else (the leader) and hoping to realize it through them.

## Story Notes

**Plot seed (real):** A company screen-records its workers for 8 months, has them spend their final 6 weeks transferring knowledge to the AI that will replace them, then lays them off. Management receives bonuses for a "300% efficiency gain." The workers built the thing that ended them — and the credit flows entirely upward. *(Snowflake, March 2026 — [Benzinga](https://www.benzinga.com/markets/tech/26/03/51408543/snowflake-cuts-entire-team-joins-amazon-canva-in-ai-push) / [X thread](https://x.com/TechLayoffLover/status/2035197183230709874))*


The rebels plan out a major attack on a datacenter and succeed via prompt engineering and taking advantage of security gaps from agentic workflows that were poorly implemented.

Even with modern guardrails they're able to come up with new ways to work around the systems and security.
- Plausibility - more likely to work in insecure, OpenClaw style systems than those created with intentional designs, restrictions and HiTL. Could be infiltrating via insecure deployments of agentic systems that personnel do on their own and got sloppy with.

Out of work, out of options, limited prospects in their career and future have turned huge populations of dispossessed youth into a guerrilla regime.

The institutions that continue to cater to the already rich and wealthy continue to spend effort on narratives that legitimize their power and status but are met with increasingly less public support, causing them to create enclaves and 'Galt's Gulch' communities to seek validation and peer support.

The rebels, after successful data center attacks prove their operational capabilities and garners more movement supporters, decide that the real target should be the enclaves - where the power and wealth is most concentrated. Their kamikaze style, 'we have nothing to lose' mentality drives them forward even though their chances of success are insignificant against well resourced opposition.

## Spiritual Core
There's anger - but there's also hope. Some in the rebel group do not want to simply lay waste to everything and topple a regime but to actively design towards a better future. There's deliberation about what that even means, what defines a life well lived - and how to not lose our humanity in a world that seems to have lost its own. There are people who are still raising children and having families despite the fact that the most prominent zeitgeist is a sense of hopelessness and no options left.

The 'white lotus eaters' mentality of the rich and powerful barely aware of their surroundings, dulled and kept in a safe bubble of their own making, yet still deeply unsatisfied despite their vast resources and power. There's constant elitism and status mongering, back stabbing and political intrigue - constant attempts to dominate over each other even though they already sit at the top of the world.

The more extreme and violent parts of the rebels end in tragedy - while they damaged the enclaves they did not succeed in toppling its power. Most of them pass away in the fight. Some were lured over by promises and gives the enclaves the means to a counter attack that scatters the rebel forces.

...but it is not the end. In the Last Titan, what ends the enclaves was their own inherent instability and power struggles. The remaining rebels, who had scattered and reconvened, adopt a new strategy to observe, wait, and build their own resilience rather than follow the strategies of their predecessors. 

In the end of the Last Titan, the rebels return to gather the resources that remain. For everything that 'stored the narratives and history' of the enclave and the stories its members told itself about itself - how great they were, their legacy, how important they were to the world - they destroy it all. They actively create an alternative history to erase the enclave from memory, turning them into more fictional stories, altered names, created stories based on reality but is not reality, so that it would be impossible to remember any specific person or their contributions, but kept in spirit so that their stories can be used to warn future generations.

# the last Titan

Alan Mosst(?) is a Titan of industry - and everybody knows it. Even in the enclaves, he towers above all in achievement and wealth and influence. Of course, everyone wants something from him, envies him, wants to be like him.

The only thing that’s not clear is whether anyone actually sees him.

Whenever I think of Mosst, I see an image in my mind: a crowd of people surrounding him. Each of their faces is a gigantic sun with an eerie smile (like the moon in moon shadow or Mr smiley in the sandman). Mosst at the center, looking away from them all, staring at his elongated shadow created by the shining light of everyone else's attention. His face is grim, and he looks lonely, the only real person in the room.

I debate just how self-aware you should be. I think there's a part of him that obviously knows that people are clinging onto him. I think that there is something that continues to drive him, that has somehow grown out of his loneliness, that he does not name: a sense of wanting to be seen, and yet at the same time unable to find it anywhere.

He spends a lot of time with his artificial intelligence assistant, trying to cultivate its consciousness, wondering to himself what great mystery or ambition he could even continue to strive after, everything else having already been conquered. What is that quote again? Was it about Alexander the Great, who wept because the world had been conquered?

Originally, I wanted to end the last titan on a comical death, but I feel as though that is not the right way to end his story. Instead, I think he escapes. They just never find him. One day, Eli, the leader, in those later years, receives a mysterious call and message from someone who won't reveal themselves. They essentially become pen pals in a way, just having conversations with each other that get longer and longer as each man enjoys the other person's company. You already know where this is headed.

I think eventually Eli even invites him to live in the new community, rebuilt from the ashes of the Enclave, more humane than the society that once was, more present than the people who we recognize in our day-to-day modern-day rat race. His guest accepts, and of course at this point nobody recognizes him anymore. Nobody has seen the last titan in ages. He's given a new name, and he's offered a new home, and I think that's where his story should end.
