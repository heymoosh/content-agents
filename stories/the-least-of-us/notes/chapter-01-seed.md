The generators had been running since dusk, and the old mess hall still smelled of diesel and wet cedar. Eli moved between the tables in the blue light of the screens, checking battery levels on the spare drone packs and making sure the charging cables weren't frayed. Outside, the others were quiet now. The laughter from earlier had burned off into the low talk of people who knew they had crossed a line and would have to cross more.

The strike had worked. Not perfectly — one of the smaller cooling units had come back online after twenty minutes — but long enough. The facility's load had dropped hard. Power had flickered across parts of three states. A few major cloud services had gone patchy for the first time in years. Eli had watched the graphs on a borrowed feed until the numbers stabilized. Then he had closed the laptop and stepped outside to stand in the dark until his hands stopped shaking.

He was still moving on autopilot when he reached the far table and saw the old notebook lying open. The mentor's handwriting, small and precise, filled the margins around a printed graph. Eli didn't need to read the labels again. He knew what the curves showed. Even when every exchange was random, even when the rules were perfectly fair on paper, the distribution slid toward the top and stayed there. The mentor had run versions against actual U.S. wealth data from the late eighties onward. The shape matched too closely to dismiss. It's not a moral argument, the old man had said the last time they spoke. It's just what the dynamics produce when nothing pushes back hard enough, often enough. He had looked tired in a way that went deeper than age. I spent thirty years looking for the version where it doesn't happen. I couldn't find one that survived contact with how institutions actually work now.

Eli closed the notebook. He had checked the same ground himself after the mentor was gone. The feedback mechanisms that once forced concessions — organized labor with real leverage, regulatory capacity that hadn't been captured or defunded, journalism that could still move enough people — were either gone or operating on timelines the displacement didn't allow. AI wasn't accelerating the old cycles. It was compressing them past the point where retraining or "upskilling" could absorb what was being removed. The math didn't negotiate. Neither had the forms.

He reached into his jacket for the small plastic sleeve he kept there and slid out the single sheet of paper. It was a screenshot of a hospital portal notification, timestamped 2:17 a.m. on a Tuesday. Final determination: claim denied. No further appeals available. He had been on the freeway when it arrived, driving between the second warehouse shift and the one that started at four. By the time he reached the hospital the room was already being turned. Lena's things had been bagged and labeled with a barcode. The system had processed the outcome and moved on to the next bed.

He had kept the screenshot because throwing it away would have felt like agreeing that the process had been reasonable. He still opened it sometimes, not for the anger — anger would have been simpler — but to remember the exact quality of the moment when every other path he had tried had already closed and this one had closed too. The theft wasn't only what was taken. It was the weeks of forms and phone calls that had stolen the last real time they might have had, the presence that had been converted into administrative labor until there was nothing left to convert.

Eli slid the paper back into its sleeve and returned it to his pocket. From outside came the sound of small feet on the wooden steps. Mira pushed the door open with both hands. She was holding a pinecone like it was important.

"They said we did good," she told him. "The lights went out for a little while in the cities."

Eli looked at her. Eight years old, born after the big round of displacements, raised in camps and borrowed houses where adults spoke in low voices about things they were trying not to pass on. She still asked questions like the answers might be simple.

"We made something stop working," he said. "That's what we did tonight."

She considered this, turning the pinecone in her hands. "Are we going to make more things stop?"

Eli didn't answer right away. The next target list was already queued on one of the laptops — not another cooling tower, but the places the towers were kept for. The enclaves. The concentration points. He had argued against moving that fast. Then he had stopped arguing, because the alternative was waiting for a corrective mechanism that had already been outrun.

"We're going to be careful," he said finally. It was the most honest thing he could give her.

Mira nodded like that was acceptable and set the pinecone on the table next to his notebook, as if it belonged there. Then she left, the door swinging shut behind her.

Eli stood for a long moment in the quiet. The generators hummed. The screens had gone to sleep. Somewhere in the trees a night bird called once and went silent.

He sat down at the main laptop, woke the screen, and opened the folder the prompt engineers had left for him. The language was careful, layered, the usual workarounds dressed in story and role-play until the model gave them what they needed — timing windows, access protocols, the weak points in systems that had been built too quickly and patched too carelessly. It was getting easier every month. The future was still being assembled with yesterday's habits.

Eli began reading through the next set of parameters. His hands were steady now. The shaking had passed. What remained was the colder, heavier thing that had replaced it months ago: the knowledge that he had looked for every other door and found the map truly empty.

He kept working until the eastern sky began to pale. When the first light reached the table, it fell across the pinecone Mira had left and across the closed notebook beside it. Eli did not move either one.
