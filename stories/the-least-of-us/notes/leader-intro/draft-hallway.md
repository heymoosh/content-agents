Eli heard the laugh before he saw the light.

It came from the far end of the hall, the short, disbelieving kind that happens when you've been holding your breath for months and the air finally moves.

He was already moving.

The generator room door was open two inches and a sliver of white was leaking into the dark.

He pulled it shut without speaking, and whoever was inside went quiet.

He stood with his back to the door for three seconds, listening to the tree line.

Nothing answered.

He walked back toward the mess hall, the gravel quiet under his boots, and tried to button his jacket cuff with one hand.

The button slipped.

He tried again and it slipped again and he put both hands in his pockets instead.

Through the streaked window he could see the thermal feed on the laptop screen, muted, looping, the cooling towers blooming white and then going dark, white and dark, forty-one minutes of regional flicker that the early crawl at the bottom was calling "unexplained infrastructure stress."

He had watched it once.

He did not need to watch it again.

What he needed was for the six people outside to behave as though they had not just done what they had just done.

He pushed through the side door.

---

Dez was crouched at the far end of the hall beside two grounded drones and a coil of cable, working the battery latch on the nearer one with a flathead he kept palming like a card trick.

"You're going to strip it," Eli said.

"I'm not going to strip it."

Eli crouched across from him and held the frame steady.

Dez got the latch and pulled the cell and set it on the concrete and immediately started on the second one.

"We're doing the generator next," Eli said.

"After the cable."

"Generator first."

Dez looked up briefly, then back at the latch.

"You heard the laugh," he said.

"I heard it."

"That's Reyes."

"He gets like that. It means nothing."

"It means he's not thinking about what happens if we get loud tonight."

Dez worked the second cell free and set it beside the first.

"They'll be looking at the grid fault," he said.

"Not us. They're not even looking at us yet."

"That's the part that worries me."

Dez sat back on his heels.

His hands were still moving, turning the flathead over and over.

"Four states were watching the same news crawl at the same time," he said.

"That's forty million people who now know the grid is softer than anyone said."

"That's not nothing."

"It's something," Eli said. "I'm not saying it's nothing."

"But."

"But they didn't think we could do anything."

"That's the only reason the door was open."

"You understand that."

"The disbelief was the mechanism."

"The door closes now."

Dez started coiling cable, hand over hand, fast.

"Or we go through a different door," he said.

Eli reached over and took the other end of the cable so the coil wouldn't tangle.

"You're talking about the enclaves," he said.

"I'm talking about where the power actually sits."

"Not the pipes. The source."

"The source has walls, Dez."

"The source has walls and also three hundred infrastructure contracts that have to talk to the outside world. You think they hardened those?"

Eli did not answer right away.

The generator hummed in the next room.

A piece of him, smaller than he liked and more honest than he wanted, had already done the same arithmetic.

"One door opened," he said.

"One time. We're not reading one door as a road."

"It wasn't one door."

Dez stopped coiling.

"That's what I'm telling you."

"I've been looking at the surface area."

"The systems they built to be the future, they're still being built."

"Fast and careless and by people who are certain they've already won."

"There's not one door, Eli."

"There's a hallway."

Eli kept his hands on the cable.

"I know," he said.

"Then why are we talking about restraint."

"Because I don't trust either of us right now."

Dez looked at him.

For a moment his hands were still.

Then he went back to coiling, faster than before.

"Generator," Eli said, and stood.

---

He did not hear Mira come in.

He was at the near table, unwinding a knotted zip tie that had gotten into the cable bag, and when he looked up she was just inside the door, holding a stick she had been using as a sword and a closed pinecone in the other hand, watching him with the flat attention children give to things they are deciding about.

"You're supposed to be in the tent," he said.

"Yolanda's snoring."

"Yolanda snores every night."

"I know," Mira said. "I can usually sleep through it."

She came closer and looked at the cable coil and the drones and the generator power strip Eli had pulled from the wall.

"Did we win?" she said.

Eli set down the zip tie.

He thought about what was true and small enough to give her.

"We made them look," he said.

She considered this.

"Look where?"

"At what they assumed they didn't have to watch."

"Is that winning?"

"Winning takes longer," he said.

"Tonight we made them look. That's what tonight was."

She nodded, not as if she was satisfied but as if she was storing it.

She walked to the long table where the mentor's closed notebook sat and set the pinecone down beside it, deliberately, as if it belonged there.

She looked at both objects for a moment.

"Goodnight," she said.

"Goodnight, Mira."

She went back out through the same door she had come in, and he heard nothing.

---

He stood looking at the pinecone and the notebook for a moment longer than he meant to.

Then his hand went into his jacket.

The plastic sleeve was in the interior pocket, where it always was, worn soft at the corners from being taken out and put back.

He did not take it all the way out.

He held it through the fabric and felt its shape, the folded weight of it, and then he did take it out and held it between two fingers without unfolding it.

He knew what it said.

Final determination: claim denied. No further appeals available.

Timestamped 2:17 a.m. on a Tuesday.

He had been driving between the second shift and the hospital, trying to calculate whether what was in his account could cover what had already been refused, when it came through.

By the time he got there her things were in a bag with a barcode label, and a nurse he had never seen before was stripping the bed, and the system had moved on to the next bed.

He kept it because throwing it away would feel like agreeing that the process had been reasonable.

He folded it back along its lines and put it in the sleeve and put the sleeve in the pocket.

He picked up the zip tie and started again on the knot.

---

The phone lit up on the table.

He did not pick it up immediately.

He finished the knot, which came free, and set the tie on the pile and then picked up the phone.

The message was short.

The people who worked with the systems, who mapped surfaces and measured gaps, had found another opening, and the note said it had been easier than the last one.

Not easier because they had gotten better, though they had.

Easier because the architecture was still being finished by people who were certain this phase was secure, who were iterating fast and not looking behind them, who had written the risk models before anyone like Dez was part of the calculation.

The door, the message said, was wider than the last one.

And getting wider.

Eli read it once more.

Then he set the phone face-down on the table.

He stood there with both hands flat on the table and listened to the camp, the quiet that was holding, the generator cycling down in the next room where Dez had finally cut it, the tree line still and dark.

From outside, somewhere, he heard someone walk between tents.

One set of footsteps, careful, going nowhere in a hurry.

He picked up the cable bag and walked it to the storage corner and set it on the shelf.

He came back and checked the laptop, closed the browser, let the screen go dark.

He found two more zip ties that needed sorting and sorted them.

He worked through what was left of the night at that table, small tasks, things that needed doing and could be done.

When the first light reached the table, it fell across the pinecone Mira had left and across the closed notebook beside it.

Eli did not move either one.
