# The Least of Us, Story Bible

> Living reference. The world, the rules, the major characters, the saga's shape, and the
> spiritual core. Edit it freely as the story evolves. Everything below is structured from
> Muxin's notes; the raw notes are preserved verbatim in `notes/`. Where a fact is not yet
> decided, it is marked OPEN so we do not lock it by accident.

## Logline

A near future where the math has already won. A generation locked out of the economy by
permanent, AI driven displacement turns to sabotage, led by a man who did not come to violence
from rage alone but from grief, and from a conclusion he stopped trying to argue himself out of.
The saga asks the question it refuses to answer: when a person believes every legitimate channel
has closed, what is the difference between a rebel and a tyrant, whether a door still existed that
he stopped looking for, and whether anything humane can be built from the ashes.

## The world

Near future, a continuation of 2026 rather than an extrapolation. The premises are already real;
the story just runs the logic forward.

- **Structural displacement, not a recession.** AI has permanently removed whole categories of
  labor, faster than any retraining or institution can absorb. This is not cyclical. There is no
  rate cut that fixes it. Huge populations of dispossessed youth have nothing to return to.
- **The yard sale proof.** A real kinetic wealth model (Boghosian, Tufts) shows that even under
  perfectly fair, random exchange, wealth concentrates at the top and the bottom cannot recover
  without external forced redistribution. In this world it is not a metaphor. It is a proof the
  Leader encounters and cannot refute. Oligarchy as a mathematical attractor.
- **The amplification gap.** AI does not distribute. It amplifies. The enclaves run clean, tuned,
  uncompromised models. The dispossessed get hobbled, ad driven, engagement optimized systems by
  design. The information asymmetry is structural.
- **Captured correctives.** The historical correctives (labor organizing, democratic
  redistribution, investigative journalism) have been defunded, defanged, or captured, so eroded
  that Eli can find no working one. Whether a path still exists is a question the story keeps open,
  not a fact it asserts. The world looks closed; the narrative never confirms that it is.
- **The enclaves.** As public legitimacy thins, the wealthy withdraw into fortified "Galt's
  Gulch" communities to seek validation among themselves. The "white lotus eaters": dulled,
  insulated, deeply unsatisfied despite total resources, consumed by status games and intrigue.
- **The rebels.** Out of work and out of options, dispossessed youth become a guerrilla movement.
  Their first targets are the data centers that power the system. Later, the enclaves themselves.
- **The children.** In the rebel camps, children are kept largely off technology because of the
  risk of being traced. The unintended result: they are present, joyful, and hopeful. Their
  worldview is the story's living argument that a future is still possible.

## Saga structure

One serialized series, not separate books. Two storylines run through it, the Uprising and the
Last Titan, interwoven and told together, alternating chapters only when the story calls for it
(no rigid every-other-chapter switch). The plot is loose and may evolve; this is the map.

**The Uprising thread.**
- **The wound.** Not one clean loss but a stack of concrete, personal system failures: losing the
  one he loved because a claim went unprocessed in time, years of being underpaid and working
  several jobs just to survive, and more we will keep developing. Deeply personal, never abstract.
  Morally wrong, yet legitimized at every step by ordinary real-world structures. (See "The object
  of the wound" under Open Questions.)
- **The road and the mentor.** How Eli comes to the yard sale proof and the closed loop, and the
  old, quiet mentor who tried every legitimate path and can show him where each one closed. The
  mentor shows him the map, not permission, and may be horrified by what he does with it.
- **The cell, the first strike, the turn.** The cell forms, including the Betrayer as its first
  believer. A first data center strike succeeds and the movement grows. The decision to turn on
  the enclaves. The assault: real damage, but not a toppling. The betrayal. Most rebels die or
  scatter.
- **Eli steps down.** Something happens and Eli no longer leads. Not mainly a physical injury
  (though it could be): exhaustion from the violence and the chaos, the pain of the betrayal, and
  the recognition that the original path was never going to arrive anywhere. He hands leadership
  on. What is left of the band becomes a community led collectively by community leaders, more
  democratic, no longer organized around a single personality. The collective leads now.

**The Last Titan thread.**
- **Rot from within.** The enclaves are not brought down from outside; they fall from their own
  instability and power struggles, embodied in Alan Mosst.
- **The patient strategy and the chronicle.** The scattered survivors regroup under a new
  approach: observe, wait, build resilience rather than repeat their predecessors. The erasure
  project: they rewrite the enclave's self mythology into altered, fictionalized stories so no
  specific person or legacy survives, kept only in spirit as a warning.
- **The correspondence.** Eli's later years. Mosst escapes rather than dies; they never find him.
  An anonymous pen pal reaches Eli, and the two come to enjoy each other's company across a
  growing exchange. They recognize a spiritual familiar in each other, and for the first time in a
  long time both men have the space to be curious about the alternatives that might have existed.
  The invitation. Mosst's quiet end under a new name in the humane community rebuilt from the ashes.

## Spiritual core

There is anger, but there is also hope. The story's real subject is not the war; it is what a
life well lived means in a world that seems to have lost its own, and how not to lose our
humanity while fighting something inhuman. Some rebels want only to lay waste. Others insist on
building toward something and argue about what that even is. People still raise children and make
families inside the hopelessness. The tragedy of the violent path is that it damages but does not
redeem; what finally outlasts the enclaves is presence, patience, and the refusal to become the
thing you fought. Eli's particular tragedy is not that no other path existed. It is that, after
his loss, he gave up looking for one and let grief and anger carry him toward an end that was
never a real destination. Only late, in the correspondence with Mosst, does the curiosity come
back, the chance to wonder what the alternatives might have been.

## Themes and touchstones

These are lenses for weight, not references to drop inside the prose.

- **Tragic, not monstrous.** A monster acts from appetite alone (rage, revenge). A tragic figure
  acts from grief that hardens into a conclusion. Eli is the second kind. He did the work that is
  supposed to lead away from violence, and then a loss tipped him; he stopped looking for the
  door and acted on what he had concluded. He never stops being moral; he arrives at a moral
  conclusion most people cannot follow him to. (John Brown is the archetype: right about the evil,
  convinced peaceful channels were exhausted, still unresolved as saint or terrorist. Note the
  word convinced. The story does not certify that the channels were truly gone, only that he
  believed it.)
- **The Rebel (Camus).** The line between saying no to a specific injustice, which affirms a human
  value, and saying no to everything, which can become its own tyranny. The story holds this open.
- **The shape of the wound (Primal, and the Black Mirror episode Common People).** The tipping
  loss must not be clean. What breaks a man who already understands the system is the specific,
  ordinary, bureaucratic way the system processes his loss: no villain in the room, no malice,
  just design running normally. The yard sale math stops being math and becomes a face. See "The
  object of the wound" under Open Questions.
- **Stolen presence.** The deepest theft in this world is not only death. It is presence: children
  unseen by parents lost to the feed, men culturally forbidden real bonds, the beautiful true
  things that ought to be common stifled and sold. Eli's grief is broad and universal, not a
  single personal grievance.

## Major characters

Full sheets live in `characters/`. In brief:

- **Eli, the Leader** (pronounced EE-lye). African American, intelligent, average height, mid to
  late 30s in the opening chapters, which find him in the aftermath of a successful attack. Did
  the intellectual work and came to the closed loop; then a loss tipped him, and grief and anger,
  not conclusion alone, are what ended his search and carried him to war. Carries an interior
  fracture (Brown's contradiction: a humanity that precludes unnecessary bloodshed, held alongside
  an armed uprising). Later he steps down: worn out by the violence and the betrayal and the
  recognition that the path led nowhere, he hands leadership to the collective.
- **The Betrayer.** Eli's first follower. An exhausted dreamer who offloaded the dream onto Eli
  rather than do the hard work of building it. Defects from burnout, not greed, and hands the
  enclaves the means to scatter the rebels. Tragic, relatable, hollow.
- **The Unwilling Prince.** An enclave insider, complicit but not evil, who saw the rot and was
  met only with denial or self exile when he tried to name it. He leaves before the end and
  becomes the rebels' chronicler, the one who can testify to how a society rots from within.
- **Alan Mosst, the Last Titan.** The most powerful man alive and the only one no one truly sees.
  His loneliness has grown into an unnamed hunger to be seen. He cultivates his AI assistant,
  searching for a remaining ambition. He escapes rather than dies, becomes Eli's anonymous pen
  pal, and ends under a new name in the new community. (Archetype intentionally rhymes with
  present day tech titans; keep him fictional.)
- **The Children.** Raised present and low tech in the camps. The story's hope made flesh.
- **Collective forces.** The Rebels; the Institution; the "prompt social engineers" who get AI to
  help them (kept strictly non operational; see craft boundary).

## Open questions (decide as we go, do not lock yet)

- **The object of the wound.** (Common People is the Black Mirror episode we are echoing, not the
  object itself.) The specific, almost mundane thing the loss leaves behind: maybe a notice that
  says the claim was denied, maybe the medical band his loved one wore. It is not only a token of
  the person he lost. It is the symbol of the systemic rot, the thing into which he pours his
  grief and anger. We will know the exact object when we find it.
- **The pause before conclusion.** Eli's cliff edge moment (the Primal beat) where another path
  still existed. Who or what could have been his Fang, the one who might have said "I see you,
  here is something worth staying for," and how he turned away from it. The point is not that the
  path was sealed; it is that, in his grief, he stopped trying to find it.
- **The mentor.** Likely a very old, very quiet former regulator or economist who tried every
  legitimate path and can show exactly where each one closed for him. Shows the map, not
  permission. May be horrified by what Eli does. The "I showed you the truth, not permission"
  tension.
- Names for the Betrayer, the Unwilling Prince, and the children.

## Craft boundary (non negotiable for this series)

The rebels' methods are rendered at the level of character, stakes, and consequence. We can draw
on examples and techniques that are already widely shared publicly (reported incidents, the kind
of thing in the "8 of 10 chatbots" reporting, social engineering that is already common knowledge)
to keep the world credible. What we do not do is invent novel operational methodology or assemble
a usable, step-by-step recipe for attacking real or realistic systems. When a scene turns on the
exact "how," abstract it: show the nerves, the cost, the morality, the aftermath. This is also the
better fiction; the engine of this story is grief, not tactics.
