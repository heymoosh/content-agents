# The Least of Us, Story Bible

> Living reference. The world, the rules, the major characters, the saga's shape, and the
> spiritual core. Edit it freely as the story evolves. Everything below is structured from
> Muxin's notes; the raw notes are preserved verbatim in `notes/`. Where a fact is not yet
> decided, it is marked OPEN so we do not lock it by accident.

## Logline

A near future where the math has already won. A generation locked out of the economy by
permanent, AI driven displacement turns to sabotage, led by a man who did not come to violence
from rage but from conclusion. He was looking for a door out and could not find one. The saga
asks the question it refuses to answer: when every legitimate channel has truly closed, what is
the difference between a rebel and a tyrant, and can anything humane be built from the ashes.

## The world

Near future, a continuation of 2026 rather than an extrapolation. The premises are already real;
the story just runs the logic forward.

- **Structural displacement, not a recession.** AI has permanently removed whole categories of
  labor, faster than any retraining or institution can absorb. This is not cyclical. There is no
  rate cut that fixes it. Huge populations of dispossessed youth have nothing to return to.
- **The yard sale proof.** A real kinetic wealth model (Boghosian, Tufts) shows that even under
  perfectly fair, random exchange, wealth concentrates at the top and the bottom cannot recover
  without external forced redistribution. In this world it is not a metaphor. It is a proof the
  Leader encounters and cannot refute. Oligarchy as a mathematical attractor.
- **The amplification gap.** AI does not distribute. It amplifies. The enclaves run clean, tuned,
  uncompromised models. The dispossessed get hobbled, ad driven, engagement optimized systems by
  design. The information asymmetry is structural.
- **Captured correctives.** The historical correctives (labor organizing, democratic
  redistribution, investigative journalism) have been defunded, defanged, or captured. There is
  no internal corrective mechanism left. The system has outrun the capacity of its own brakes.
- **The enclaves.** As public legitimacy thins, the wealthy withdraw into fortified "Galt's
  Gulch" communities to seek validation among themselves. The "white lotus eaters": dulled,
  insulated, deeply unsatisfied despite total resources, consumed by status games and intrigue.
- **The rebels.** Out of work and out of options, dispossessed youth become a guerrilla movement.
  Their first targets are the data centers that power the system. Later, the enclaves themselves.
- **The children.** In the rebel camps, children are kept largely off technology because of the
  risk of being traced. The unintended result: they are present, joyful, and hopeful. Their
  worldview is the story's living argument that a future is still possible.

## Saga structure

A serialized saga in (at least) two books. The plot is loose and may evolve; this is the map.

- **Book 1, The Uprising.** Eli's intellectual road to conclusion and the mentor who shows him a
  map with no doors. The personal, system processed loss that collapses the distance between what
  he knew and what he feels. The cell forms. A first data center strike succeeds and the movement
  grows. The decision to turn on the enclaves. The assault: real damage, but not a toppling. The
  betrayal. Most rebels die or scatter.
- **Book 2, The Last Titan.** The enclaves are not brought down from outside; they fall from
  their own instability and power struggles, embodied in Alan Mosst. The scattered rebels regroup
  under a new strategy: observe, wait, build resilience rather than repeat their predecessors. The
  erasure project: they rewrite the enclave's self mythology into altered, fictionalized stories
  so no specific person or legacy survives, kept only in spirit as a warning. Eli's later years.
  The anonymous pen pal who turns out to be Mosst, the invitation, and Mosst's quiet end under a
  new name in the humane community rebuilt from the ashes.

## Spiritual core

There is anger, but there is also hope. The story's real subject is not the war; it is what a
life well lived means in a world that seems to have lost its own, and how not to lose our
humanity while fighting something inhuman. Some rebels want only to lay waste. Others insist on
building toward something and argue about what that even is. People still raise children and make
families inside the hopelessness. The tragedy of the violent path is that it damages but does not
redeem; what finally outlasts the enclaves is presence, patience, and the refusal to become the
thing you fought.

## Themes and touchstones

These are lenses for weight, not references to drop inside the prose.

- **Tragic, not monstrous.** A monster acts from appetite (rage, revenge). A tragic figure acts
  from conclusion. Eli is the second kind. He did the work that is supposed to lead away from
  violence and arrived somewhere he did not want to be. He never stops being moral; he arrives at
  a moral conclusion most people cannot follow him to. (John Brown is the archetype: right about
  the evil, right that peaceful channels were exhausted, still unresolved as saint or terrorist.)
- **The Rebel (Camus).** The line between saying no to a specific injustice, which affirms a human
  value, and saying no to everything, which can become its own tyranny. The story holds this open.
- **The shape of the wound (Primal, Common People).** The tipping loss must not be clean. What
  breaks a man who already understands the system is the specific, ordinary, bureaucratic way the
  system processes his loss: no villain in the room, no malice, just design running normally. The
  yard sale math stops being math and becomes a face. See "The crib" under Open Questions.
- **Stolen presence.** The deepest theft in this world is not only death. It is presence: children
  unseen by parents lost to the feed, men culturally forbidden real bonds, the beautiful true
  things that ought to be common stifled and sold. Eli's grief is broad and universal, not a
  single personal grievance.

## Major characters

Full sheets live in `characters/`. In brief:

- **Eli, the Leader.** Acts from conclusion, not appetite. Did the intellectual work, found no
  door, then suffered a loss the system processed without ever seeing a face. Carries an interior
  fracture (Brown's contradiction: a humanity that precludes unnecessary bloodshed, held alongside
  an armed uprising). Name OPEN between Eli (primary) and Ren.
- **The Betrayer.** Eli's first follower. An exhausted dreamer who offloaded the dream onto Eli
  rather than do the hard work of building it. Defects from burnout, not greed, and hands the
  enclaves the means to scatter the rebels. Tragic, relatable, hollow.
- **The Unwilling Prince.** An enclave insider, complicit but not evil, who saw the rot and was
  met only with denial or self exile when he tried to name it. He leaves before the end and
  becomes the rebels' chronicler, the one who can testify to how a society rots from within.
- **Alan Mosst, the Last Titan.** The most powerful man alive and the only one no one truly sees.
  His loneliness has grown into an unnamed hunger to be seen. He cultivates his AI assistant,
  searching for a remaining ambition. He escapes rather than dies, becomes Eli's anonymous pen
  pal, and ends under a new name in the new community. (Archetype intentionally rhymes with
  present day tech titans; keep him fictional.)
- **The Children.** Raised present and low tech in the camps. The story's hope made flesh.
- **Collective forces.** The Rebels; the Institution; the "prompt social engineers" who get AI to
  help them (kept strictly non operational; see craft boundary).

## Open questions (decide as we go, do not lock yet)

- **The crib.** Eli's equivalent of the Common People crib: the small, almost mundane object or
  act that represents the future that was never allowed to exist, consumed by the system to keep
  running. We will know it when we find it. Not the death itself; the shape of what came after.
- **The pause before conclusion.** Eli's cliff edge moment (the Primal beat) where another path
  still existed. Who or what could have been his Fang, the one who might have said "I see you,
  here is something worth staying for," and how that door closed.
- **The mentor.** Likely a very old, very quiet former regulator or economist who tried every
  legitimate path and can show exactly where each one closed. Shows the map, not permission. May
  be horrified by what Eli does. The "I showed you the truth, not permission" tension.
- Names for the Betrayer, the Unwilling Prince, and the children.
- Eli's physical description and age (notes are light here; sketch lightly, confirm with Muxin
  before locking into canon).

## Craft boundary (non negotiable for this series)

The rebels' methods are rendered at the level of character, stakes, and consequence, never as
real instruction. No working attack methodology against real or realistic systems, and no real
AI jailbreak or prompt injection technique. When a scene turns on "how," abstract it: show the
nerves, the cost, the morality, the aftermath, not a usable recipe. This is also the better
fiction; the engine of this story is grief and conclusion, not tactics.
