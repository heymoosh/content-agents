On the center screen a thermal loop played on mute: the data center's cooling towers blooming bright, then cooling, then going dark.
Forty-one minutes of regional flicker.
Three states had felt it.
The news was already calling it "unexplained infrastructure stress."

Eli stood with his arms at his sides and watched the loop run again.

The mess hall smelled of diesel and wet cedar, the generators still turning over outside, and the blue light of the screens made everything look like it was underwater.
He had expected to feel something clean when it worked.
What he felt instead was the removal of a pressure he had almost stopped noticing.
His hands had been shaking in the hour before, badly enough that he'd gone outside and stood in the dark with his palms flat against the wall of the building until they stopped.
He had not told anyone.

He moved between the tables now, checking battery levels on the spare drone packs, making sure the charging cables weren't frayed.
Work was something he could still do right.

The others were still outside in the dark, voices low and electric.
Someone laughed, the short, disbelieving kind that happens when you've been holding your breath for months and the air finally moves.
He could hear them through the thin wall, that particular frequency of people who knew they had crossed a line and would have to cross more.

None of them had chosen this camp the way you choose a place.
Darius, who was twenty-six, had spent three years as a logistics coordinator until the route optimization software made his position a rounding error; he still moved through rooms like he was scheduling something.
Priya was twenty-two and had a degree in environmental systems she had finished remotely from her parents' basement because the internship track evaporated the semester before she graduated.
The oldest was forty-one, a man called Reyes who had worked demolition until the rebar-setting robots came in and the crew shrank from thirty to four overnight; he had driven his truck eleven hours to get here, and he never talked about what he had left behind.
They had arrived by different roads, through different failures, and the camp was what they had in common: a place people ended up.
The logging operation the buildings had belonged to was two years closed.
Someone had left a payroll calendar on the wall of the main hall, weeks X'd out in pencil through the fall, ending in a month with no X's at all.
Nobody had taken it down.

The drones were dark tonight, grounded and stowed; running them after an operation was how cells got found.
The camp ran quiet by habit anyway, lights low, RF discipline after nine.
The children were the main reason for the last rule: they used the radio band for nothing, because the children used nothing, no tablets, no feeds, no personal devices of any kind.
The risk of being traced was the reason, and everyone understood it.
What nobody had planned for was what grew in the absence: Mira and the four others who had been born into the camps or brought here young were present in a way that unsettled adults who had spent years inside their own attention.
They made eye contact when they asked questions.
They finished their sentences.

The strike had worked.
It was the first one that had.
Not perfectly: one of the smaller cooling units had come back online after twenty minutes.
But long enough.
The facility's load had dropped hard, and the cascade had done the rest.

He found the mentor's notebook open on a table near the far wall.

He did not pick it up.
He just looked at it, the way you look at a thing that once took a long time to understand.
The mentor had filled it over thirty years: margins dense, the handwriting getting smaller as the arguments got larger.
He had spent those thirty years looking for the version where the distribution does not slide toward the top and stay there.
Every system he had tried, every corrective he had watched work briefly before getting absorbed, every law that made it two years before the exceptions metastasized.
He had shown Eli the map not as permission but as information, and two years ago he had died without finding the door he was looking for.

Eli had thought, for a long time, that he would find it instead.

He had tried the unions that no longer had leverage.
He had tried the votes that arrived pre-bought, the long articles that disappeared behind paywalls, the code submissions that got throttled or forked into something harmless.
He had even tried, for a while, believing that if he just worked harder, borrowed less, stayed smaller, the math might make an exception.
It had not.
Not for him, and not for Lena, and not for the people in the camps who had done everything right and arrived at forty with nothing.
He had stopped looking for the door not because he proved it wasn't there, but because one morning he realized he no longer believed it was.

He kept a screenshot in a plastic sleeve in the breast pocket of his jacket.
He opened it sometimes, not to remind himself of the injustice, but to remember the exact texture of the moment the last door had clicked shut while he was driving to pay for the care that had already been refused.
It was a screenshot of a hospital portal notification, timestamped 2:17 a.m. on a Tuesday.
Final determination: claim denied.
No further appeals available.
He had been on the freeway when it arrived, driving between the second warehouse shift and the one that started at four.
By the time he reached the hospital the room was already being turned.
Lena's things had been bagged and labeled with a barcode.
The system had processed the outcome and moved on to the next bed.

He had kept the screenshot because throwing it away would have felt like agreeing that the process had been reasonable.

He had not thrown it away.

She had believed, even at the end, that people were capable of more than what they had organized themselves to do.
She said it plainly, the way she said most things, without performing it.
He had loved that about her.
He did not know how to hold it now.

A small hand tugged Eli's sleeve.

Mira, eight, maybe nine, stood there with pine needles stuck to her knees and a stick she'd been using as a sword.
She had come in from the dark without him hearing the door.

"Did we win?" she asked.

He looked at her for a moment.
She was watching him with the particular patience of children who have learned that adults take longer to answer than the question deserves.

"We made them look," he said.

She considered that.
The stick hung loose in one hand.
In the other she had a pinecone, the kind with the tight scales, still closed.
She held it out and then set it on the table next to the notebook, not offering it, just placing it there, as if it had always belonged.

"Look at what?" she said.

"At something they thought they didn't have to look at."

"Is that the same as winning?"

"It's the beginning of something," he said.
"Winning takes longer."

She nodded slowly, working it through.
She did not seem afraid.
She never seemed afraid, and he had stopped wondering whether that was innocence or something stronger.

"Are we going to do it again?" she asked.

"We'll be careful," he said.
"We'll think about it first."

"You always think about things first."

"Yes."

She looked at the screens, at the thermal loop still turning.
She watched the towers bloom and cool and go dark.
"I like that it's quiet out there," she said.
"The mountains, I mean.
Not the machines."

"Me too," he said.

She went back out the way she came, trailing the stick, and the door swung shut behind her on its pneumatic hinge.

He stood there for a moment in the after-quiet.

Then his phone lit up on the table.

A new message from the prompt engineers: they'd found another sloppy agentic deployment, some mid-level contractor's workflow left open, guardrails bypassed with the usual metaphors and role-play until the model gave them what they needed.
It had been easy this season, all of it, because nobody who mattered believed people like them could do anything at all.
The systems that were supposed to be the future were still being built like it was 2023.

He read it and set the phone face-down.

That was the part that would not last.
Tonight had worked because it was the first, because the place had been guarded against weather and theft and bored teenagers, not against them.
After this the cameras would multiply and the contracts would tighten and somewhere a line item would open for the specific problem of people like him.
The next facility would not look anything like this one.
He had said so to the cell, twice, carefully: that the easy version of this closed the moment they used it, that they would have to be faster and stranger, and never the same way twice.
They had listened.
He was less sure they had felt it.
He could already hear it in the voices through the wall, the appetite to go again and go bigger, and he did not trust the appetite, and not trusting it had never once made it quieter.

He kept working.
He checked the last of the cable connections and logged the battery levels and noted two packs that would need replacement before the week was out.
He worked until the sky outside the high windows began to shift, the dark thinning at the edges.

When the first light reached the table, it fell across the pinecone Mira had left and across the closed notebook beside it.
Eli did not move either one.
