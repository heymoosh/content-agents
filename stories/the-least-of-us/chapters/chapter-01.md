The go-live was scheduled for 6 a.m., which meant Marcus was at his desk by four.

The office was dark except for the monitor glow and the thin strip of light under the server room door, and he sat in that quiet and ran the pre-flight one more time.

Not because he doubted the system.

Because Hendricks would ask, and Marcus wanted the answer ready before the question.

The adjudication engine had processed eleven thousand test claims in the past month at an accuracy rate of 97.3 percent, which was four points above the human review average and fourteen times faster.

He had built most of it.

Not alone, but the core decision tree was his, the appeal-routing logic was his, the confidence thresholds he'd argued for in three successive architecture reviews were his.

He was proud of it, in the way you're proud of something hard you finished.

By five-thirty the room had filled: Hendricks and two of his VPs standing near the window in their good shirts, a compliance officer Marcus didn't recognize, and the human review team arranged around the long table with the particular stillness of people who already know.

Reza was at the far end, near the door.

Marcus had worked two floors above Reza for three years without overlapping, but he knew the face: the one who always had coffee already when the meeting started, who laughed easily and then went quiet and actually listened.

He was listening now.

"We good?" Hendricks said, not to the room, to Marcus.

"We're good," Marcus said.

He pulled up the console and typed the command he'd typed a hundred times in staging, and hit enter, and the system went live.

The first claim hit the queue in four seconds.

Status: reviewed.

Determination: approved.

Processing time: 0.8 seconds.

The VP on the left said something quietly approving and Hendricks said "there it is" and someone started a small applause that the room didn't fully commit to.

Marcus watched the queue.

Claims in, determinations out, the rhythm clean and fast and indifferent.

By six-fifteen the backlog that the human team had been working since November was gone.

He felt the shape of it then: not guilt exactly, something less defined, a noticing.

He looked up once, toward Reza's end of the table.

Reza was looking at his phone.

The layoff notices went out at nine, by email, from an HR address Marcus had never seen before.

He learned this later, secondhand, because he was in a debrief with Hendricks when it happened.

He told himself it was progress, which was true.

He told himself the work he'd done was real, which was also true.

He did not think very hard about the gap between those two things.

He got home at seven with the good bread from the place on Foster, which was his contribution to nights when Dana cooked.

She had made the chicken with the olives, Sam's current favorite, and the kitchen smelled like garlic and something bright, and Sam was at the table doing something with crayons that had colonized the entire surface.

"Move, bug," Marcus said, and kissed the top of Sam's head.

"I'm almost done," Sam said, which had been true for forty minutes according to Dana's expression.

The three of them ate.

Dana asked how the go-live went and Marcus said fine, good, clean.

She said "that's good" in the voice she used when she thought there was more and was giving him room for it, and he didn't take the room, and she let it go.

Sam held up the drawing: a figure in something that might have been a spacesuit or a lab coat, the decision unclear.

"Scientist," Sam announced.

"You've been a scientist three times," Marcus said.

"This is a different scientist."

"What does this scientist do?"

Sam considered this with the seriousness the question deserved.

"She finds things."

Dana smiled without looking up from the plates she was stacking.

"You can be anything," Marcus said.

Sam took the drawing back and studied it.

"Even if it's something they haven't invented yet?"

"Especially that," he said.

He meant it.

That was the thing, at the time: he meant it completely.

The bread was good, the kitchen was warm, Sam fell asleep on the couch before eight and he carried Sam to bed, and Dana poured two glasses of wine and they talked about nothing important until eleven, and he went to sleep satisfied in the uncomplicated way of a man who'd done a hard thing well.

The ritual they fell into, after that night, was the bread.

The specific bread, the one from Foster, on nights that called for it: good days, hard days, days that needed marking.

Dana would set it on the cutting board and they'd tear it rather than slice it, which Sam thought was funny, bread with their hands like animals, and it became the thing they did.

He thought about this later, the way you always think about the last time you didn't know it was the last time.

The email arrived on a Tuesday, which felt like a detail that should mean something.

The subject line was "Important: Next Steps for Your Role" and it was from his manager, whose name was at the bottom, signed, though Marcus later understood the text had been generated and the manager had approved it without writing a word.

There was a calendar invite for ten a.m.

He accepted it.

He went to the meeting, which was fifteen minutes, and which included his manager and someone from HR, and the words they used were "workforce realignment" and "role elimination" and "we want to support your transition," and when it was over he walked back to his desk and found that his badge still worked but his laptop had already been remotely locked.

The screen said to return the hardware to the front desk.

He sat there for a moment in front of the locked screen.

Someone from the team walked past and looked at him and looked away.

He thought about the claims queue, the determinations flowing out at 0.8 seconds each.

He thought about the confidence thresholds he'd argued for.

He understood, then, in a way he hadn't on the day of the go-live: not the economics, not some abstract sense of irony, just the plain mechanical fact that the logic which had processed eleven thousand claims had a name for what he was now.

Case type: workforce.

Determination: no longer required.

He returned the laptop.

He drove home and sat in the car for a while before going inside.

The applications took three weeks to learn.

Not to write, to understand: the systems behind the forms, the screening logic, the way you submitted your materials to a portal that confirmed receipt and then produced nothing, no rejection, no contact, no evidence that a human had seen any of it.

He knew what was on the other side.

He had built something like it.

He submitted forty applications in the first month.

Then sixty.

He rewrote his resume four times based on advice from a service that charged him three hundred dollars and returned a document with bullet points that didn't sound like him, and the applications kept going into the portals and the portals kept confirming receipt.

At ninety-seven applications he got a phone screen, automated, a voice asking him to answer questions about project management in two minutes per question, his answers recorded and then scored by something he couldn't see.

He didn't hear back.

Sam, that summer, had moved on from scientists.

"Coach," Sam said.

"What kind?"

"Soccer."

"Or maybe basketball."

"Or maybe the kind that coaches coaches."

"That's called a director of player development," Marcus said.

"That's a good one," Sam said.

He was seven now, thought things through, still believed in every possibility he named.

Dana had picked up a contract position, design work, enough to cover most of the mortgage.

They didn't talk about money in a way that felt calculated: it was more that they talked around it, both of them aware of the terrain, neither of them wanting to be the one to name the shape of it to the other.

There was a reskilling program through the state workforce board.

He enrolled in January.

It was twelve weeks, cloud architecture, held in a room above a UPS store with nineteen other people in versions of his situation.

The instructor was good, actually: a contractor named Phil who knew the material and graded fairly and let class run ten minutes long if the questions were real.

Marcus finished with a certificate and a new set of acronyms on his resume and applied to sixty more positions and the portals confirmed receipt.

Two of the new positions paid less than he'd made twelve years ago, when he was starting out.

He applied anyway.

He got one of them, a contract role, three months, no benefits.

It ended at ninety days.

He filed for benefits in March.

The portal asked for the separation reason and he typed "role elimination, workforce realignment" and uploaded the termination letter and submitted the claim.

Processing time: 6 to 8 weeks.

Determination: claim under review.

Further documentation required.

He uploaded the documentation.

Determination: under review.

Second determination, twelve weeks later: claim denied.

Code 47-B: claimant's separation determined to be voluntary based on failure to complete required onboarding documentation.

He had completed every form they sent him.

He sat in the kitchen and read it twice.

He thought about the confidence thresholds.

He thought about 97.3 percent, which meant the system was wrong 2.7 percent of the time, which in eleven thousand claims was about three hundred people who got the wrong answer and had to appeal.

He appealed.

The appeal process was thirty-two pages of forms and required a notarized statement and documentation from his former employer and a review scheduled for ninety days out.

He gathered the documents.

He called the number on the form and waited forty-five minutes and spoke to someone who could not discuss the specifics of his claim.

He found a lawyer, briefly: a woman who looked at his termination agreement and pointed to page eleven, which said that any disputes arising from his employment relationship were subject to binding private arbitration and that he waived his right to class action.

"There are thousands of people from that company in the same situation," the lawyer said.

"And you can't take it to court," she said.

"And you can't group together."

He asked what he could do.

"You can go to arbitration," she said.

"Alone."

He went to arbitration, alone.

It cost him eleven hundred dollars in filing fees.

The arbitrator was a retired HR executive.

He lost.

He wrote to his representative, twice.

He got a form letter that expressed her commitment to working families and mentioned a hearing that had occurred the previous year on workforce transition policy.

He attended a town hall, stood in line, spoke for two minutes.

The representative said "I hear you" and wrote something on a notepad and moved to the next person.

He filed a complaint with the relevant agency.

Fourteen months later, a letter: the agency had reviewed the matter and found no violation of applicable law.

He read that line several times.

No violation of applicable law.

Nothing that happened to him had been illegal.

Not the automation, not the termination, not the screening, not the denial, not the arbitration clause, not the separation of him from thousands of people who could have acted together.

The system had worked exactly as designed.

It was not a failure.

It had succeeded, and he was a result.

Dana got the full-time offer in October and took it, because they needed the health insurance.

Sam, nine now, asked him once if he was going to get a new job soon.

He said yes, he was working on it.

Sam nodded and went back to whatever Sam was doing, accepting the answer at face value in the way that made Marcus want to be alone for a moment.

That winter, at the kitchen table, Dana said: "I need to know where we are."

He told her, all of it, the numbers, the timeline, the appeal, the arbitration.

She listened without interrupting.

When he was done she was quiet for a moment.

"I knew most of it," she said.

"I wanted you to tell me."

The bread from Foster was on the cutting board.

Neither of them reached for it.

The warehouse job lasted four months.

He found it through an app: register, upload your ID, complete a background check that took six hours, accept a shift.

The work was physical in a way he hadn't been in years, the body finding muscles it had forgotten, and there was something clarifying about it at first, a task with a clear end, a shelf stocked, a box moved.

He worked nights.

Dana had the mornings with Sam.

They were ships, then.

He would sometimes arrive home as she was leaving, and they'd pass in the hall, and she'd touch his arm, and he'd stand in the kitchen and eat something standing up before he slept.

It ended on a Wednesday.

He showed up to the app at six a.m. to accept his shift and found instead a notification: Account status: inactive.

Below it: review our community guidelines.

Below that: if you believe this is in error, you may submit an appeal.

He submitted an appeal through the app's form.

The form asked him to select the reason he believed his deactivation was in error.

He selected "other."

He typed three sentences.

The form said: your appeal has been submitted. Response time: 3 to 5 business days.

He never heard back.

He found out later, from another driver, that the system had flagged him for a delivery metric: door photo clarity.

The camera on his phone was slightly cracked at the corner, not enough to affect normal photos, but the system's scoring model treated it as degraded quality, and three degraded-quality photos in a week was termination.

He had not known about the scoring model.

It was not in the terms of service, not in any document he'd signed.

There was no person he could call.

He sat in the parking lot of a grocery store and looked at his phone for a while.

Then he drove home.

Dana had taken Sam to her mother's by then, a temporary arrangement that had the particular permanence of things called temporary for too long.

She called every few days.

Sam came on the phone and told him things about the cousins, the dog, a movie they'd seen.

Marcus listened and made the sounds of listening and felt the distance between them as physical, a weight in the air of the apartment.

He sold the car.

He picked up cash work when he could find it, through people he knew, jobs that paid by the day and didn't show up anywhere.

He ate carefully.

He stopped buying the bread.

The cutting board gathered a film of dust and he stopped seeing it.

He was walking back from a hardware store in November, work gloves in a bag, a job the next morning loading a van for a guy he'd met through someone from the warehouse.

The light was going.

The block had a laundromat, a check-cashing place, a fried chicken spot with the menu hand-lettered on a board in the window.

He stopped at the corner.

"Marcus."

He turned.

Reza was standing at the entrance of the laundromat, a bag over one shoulder.

Marcus hadn't seen him in three years, since the morning of the go-live, across a conference table.

Reza looked at him the way you look at someone when you are calculating how much they have changed and not wanting to show the calculation.

Marcus understood he probably looked worse than he thought.

"Hey," Marcus said.

They stood there for a moment.

"You want coffee?" Reza said.

"There's a place on the next block."

The coffee was bad, the place nearly empty, a woman wiping down the counter who didn't look at them.

They talked about small things for a few minutes, the shape of it, and then Reza put his cup down and said:

"I know what happened to you."

Marcus didn't say anything.

"I mean generally," Reza said.

"I know the shape of it."

"I went through my own version."

Marcus looked at the table.

"There are a lot of us," Reza said.

"People who know how the systems work from the inside."

"Who got put through them anyway."

"Or because of it."

He stopped.

"I'm not going to pitch you," he said.

"But I want you to know there are people working on something."

"Not policy."

"Not appeals."

"Not a letter to your representative."

The window was fogged from the cold outside.

Headlights moved across it.

"We want people like you," Reza said.

"Not to help you out."

"Because of what you know."

"Because the things that processed us, you understand how they think."

"Not in theory."

"From the inside."

Marcus thought about the claims queue.

The confidence thresholds.

The 2.7 percent.

He thought about the door on the appeal portal.

The door on the arbitration.

The door on the agency complaint.

The door at the town hall.

Every door in the past three years had been a wall dressed as a door, and he had knocked on all of them and they had held.

"What is it?" he said.

Reza looked at him steadily.

"People who've decided to stop asking," he said.

The woman at the counter turned off a light in the back.

Outside, the city moved on without them, its machinery indifferent and continuous, exactly as it was built to be.

Marcus looked at Reza's hand, extended across the table, and something in him recognized the gesture before his mind named it: not rescue, not salvation, nothing that simple.

A switch.

He reached out.
