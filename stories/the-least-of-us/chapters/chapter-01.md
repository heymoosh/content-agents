The fans went quiet hall by hall, each silence arriving a half-second after the last, the way a wave breaks along a shore rather than all at once.

Eli stood at the fence line and listened to the building go dark.

The cooling towers had run at a frequency you felt before you heard: a low chord, almost nautical, the sound of something enormous keeping itself alive.
Then that frequency was gone, and what replaced it was not silence but the absence of a thing you'd stopped noticing until it left.

Sodium lights still burned along the perimeter, throwing everything in yellow and shadow.
The building itself was a long, low rectangle with no windows, just louvers and concrete, a structure whose only purpose was to keep servers cold enough to think.

Eli's hands were loose at his sides.
He was watching the last two lights inside blink out.

Behind him, the others stood in a loose line against the fence, and not one of them made a sound.
That was the rule and they all knew it: you do not cheer.
Cheering was for games.

He had expected something cleaner tonight, some rearrangement inside himself.
What he felt instead was the particular stillness of a math problem solved after a long time, which is not joy so much as the removal of a specific pressure.

The heat still sat on the back of his neck.
It was early June and the land out here, between the highway and the drainage channel, had been holding heat since noon and had not yet decided to release it.
The air smelled of warm asphalt and, faintly, of the cooling chemicals the building had been bleeding off for a year; you could find them in the groundwater two miles east if you looked.
Eli had looked.

He watched one of the crew shift his weight, and another touch her face.
The discipline held.

A data center the size of this one handled the inference load for roughly eleven million daily users across four platforms.
Eli did not let himself think of it as power.
It was a number.
The number was in his chest now, where numbers lived, alongside the others he had been collecting for three years.

A footstep behind him, faster than the others: Dez.

Dez moved differently than the rest of them, with a kind of forward lean that made him look like he was always about to say something, which was usually accurate.
He was twenty-nine, with a wide face and very dark eyes and a quality of attention that could tip over into intensity without warning.
He had come to Eli first, before anyone else, and Eli still didn't entirely understand why, though he had spent time thinking about it.

"That's it," Dez said, low.
Not a question.

"That's it," Eli said.

Dez was quiet for a moment.
Eli could feel the effort it cost him.

"Next one," Dez said.
"The Phoenix facility."

"We're not doing that tonight."

"I know that."
A pause.
"But it's the next one."

Eli said nothing.

"Think about the Phoenix numbers," Dez said.
"Think about what that does to their Atlantic routing."

"I've thought about it."

The thing Eli knew about Dez, and had always known, was that Dez believed in the shape of the future more completely than any person he had ever met.
He could draw it with his hands.
He could see where the lines would run and how the pressure would move and what the world would look like after enough of the right things were taken apart.
He was almost never wrong about the shape.
He was often wrong about what came next.

"After Phoenix," Dez said, "we start talking about the enclaves."

There it was.

Eli turned to look at him.
Dez was watching the building, not Eli, and there was something in his face that was very close to satisfaction, which was not the rule against cheering exactly but was adjacent to it in a way that Eli filed for later.

"One step," Eli said.

"I know."

"One step, and then we assess."

Dez nodded, and his nod had a quality of patience that did not feel entirely patient.
He was a man agreeing to wait who was already planning what came after.

Eli turned back to the fence.

Six feet away, sitting on a piece of broken curbing just outside the circle of the crew, a girl was watching the building with the same steady attention Eli had been giving it.
She was maybe eight years old.
She had one knee up and her chin resting on it, and she was looking at the dark structure with the focused calm of a person who is simply watching a thing because the thing is there to be watched.

Her name was Sasha.
She was the daughter of one of the crew, and she had been asleep in the van when they'd arrived, which was why she was here now instead of at the camp.

She was not frightened.
She was not excited.
She was looking at the building with an unhurried attention that Eli had not seen on an adult face in longer than he could name.

He watched her watch the building.

He thought about what she was seeing: a long, dark rectangle in the dark, the sodium lights still burning at the perimeter, a silence where something large had been breathing.
He thought about what she was not seeing: the number of jobs that had run through those servers, the displacement models, the quarterly filings that showed the margin expansion that followed the cuts.

She was just looking.
He could not remember the last time he had just looked at something.

He stepped away from the fence line and moved to a patch of ground between two storage units, far enough that the others were a quiet shape in his peripheral vision.

He reached into his pocket.

The band was soft, the way plastic gets when it has been handled many times.
It was the kind of thing hospitals put on wrists, pale blue, with a bar code and a name printed in small font.
He had stopped being able to read the name in the dark a long time ago, but he turned it in his fingers anyway, the way some people carry coins or stones.

He had understood the mechanism years before it reached her.
He had written about it, had explained the logic of it to people in rooms, had laid out how the math ran and where it ended.
When the papers came, when the claim was flagged as low priority and the processing window expired and then the window closed, he had recognized every step.

He had thought: not her.
He had thought: if I work harder.
He had thought: there must be something I haven't tried.

There had not been.

He stood in the dark and turned the band in his fingers and looked at the building he had just helped go silent, and he waited for the feeling that was supposed to follow.
It did not come.
What came instead was the old tired question: right, but to what end.
He had concluded.
He had acted on the conclusion.
The conclusion was still sound and he was not sorry for it, exactly, and he was also standing in a field in the dark holding a hospital bracelet in the hot night, and those things were all true at the same time.

He put the band back in his pocket.

When he walked back to the fence line, Dez was talking to two of the crew in a low voice, and Eli could tell from the posture, the slight forward lean of all three of them, the way they were nodding, that the topic was Phoenix.
Possibly it had already moved past Phoenix.

The appetite was rising.
He could see it in the way they held their shoulders, in the energy that had nowhere to go because cheering was against the rule and the rule was necessary and the rule did not solve the problem of having won something and needing the win to mean more than it meant.

Sasha had moved from the curbing to the base of the fence.
She was pressing her fingers into the chain link, not climbing, just holding, the way children hold things to understand their shape.
She was still watching the building.

The building was still dark.

Eli stood at the edge of the group and thought about what Dez wanted and about how close it was to what the others wanted and about how wanting a thing this much was how you stopped asking whether it was the right thing.
He had seen where that led.
He knew the direction of it.

He looked at the dark building.
Eleven million users, routed elsewhere now, the query traffic absorbing seamlessly into redundant infrastructure the way water fills any shape available to it.

The building would be back online in weeks, maybe less.
The movement had been growing for three months.
The enclaves had walls and their own power and their own models, tuned clean, running fast, and nobody inside them had missed a meal because of anything Eli's people had done.

Not yet.

That was what Dez was feeling.
That was what all of them were feeling, the not-yet, the sense that they were standing at the near edge of something large enough to matter.
It was not wrong, exactly.
It was only that Eli had learned, at cost he was still carrying, the difference between the shape of a future and the weight of one.

Sasha pulled her hand back from the fence.
She looked at her fingers, then up at the sodium light.
She did not look at any of them.

Dez said his name.

Eli looked over.

Dez's face in the yellow light was open, waiting, the face of a man who has handed something over and needs the other person to hold it right.

The crew was looking at Eli now, all of them, in the way they always looked at him at the end of a thing: not for orders, exactly, but for something to carry home.

Eli said: "Good work tonight."

He said: "Get some sleep."

He did not say what he was thinking, which was that the enclaves were built to outlast exactly this.
That the appetite now moving through his people, warming them in the sodium light, was real and right and was also the most dangerous thing in the field tonight, more dangerous than the fence or the empty building or the cameras he had already accounted for.

He put his hands in his pockets.
The band was there.
He kept his hand around it and did not take it out.

They started walking back toward the van, Dez still talking, the others listening, the night still hot, the building behind them dark and silent and already, somewhere, being assessed for repair.
